I could buy the 9-series version of whatever PC part I am looking at. A 4090, a 7950X, an i9, whatever the current flagship happens to be. There is still a stubborn part of me that does not want to.

I have more fun shopping in the upper midrange.

The second and third SKU down is where the decisions are. You read too many reviews, compare the parts they cut, work out which compromises are real and which ones only exist in a benchmark, then agonize for a week longer than necessary. By the time the box arrives, you know why you bought it.

This has served me pretty well, with the rare but defensible miss. I paid about $1,000 for a 3080 12 GB as stock levels were beginning to recover. At the time it was not clear whether that was a temporary surge or the start of prices returning to earth, so I moved on it.

There is nothing wrong with the card. It was probably the only sensible option for driving my Alienware AW34 at the time, and it has done the job. I still get a small pang of superannuated buyer's remorse when I think about the price.

Buying just below the top keeps the whole thing cheerful. A flagship feels a little like handling a Fabergé egg: expensive, impressive, and vaguely stressful. Upper-midrange hardware still feels like a hobby. You can tune it, complain about it, replace it eventually, and remember that most of this stuff is also a commodity with a fan bolted to it.

---

I take the same approach when I am new to a hobby. I start with the cheaper thing because I do not yet know what the expensive thing would fix for me.

My first bike was cheap. That was fine. I needed to learn how much I actually liked riding, what kind of riding I wanted to do, and which annoyances were the bike versus which ones were just me learning to ride. Buying the end-game bike on day one would have answered none of that. It would only have made my ignorance more expensive.

A hobby is something you get better at. The gear can help, but part of the fun is developing enough taste and experience to know what “better” means for you. Otherwise you are buying somebody else's finish line and hoping the hobby appears around it.

This is not a rule against nice things. If I stay with something long enough to understand the limits of what I own, upgrading feels great. Now the spec sheet has context. I know which problem I am paying to remove, which luxury I actually notice, and which number is there to make the comparison table look tidy.

That is also what I want this reviews section to be about. I will review products, but also the decisions around them: what the extra money buys, which compromises matter after the honeymoon, and whether the cheaper option teaches you enough to make the next purchase better.
