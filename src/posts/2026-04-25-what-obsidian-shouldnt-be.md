I like Obsidian a lot. Probably too much.

That is part of the problem.

There is a certain kind of person, and I am very much one of them, who sees a flexible tool and immediately starts trying to make it the place where everything goes. Notes, obviously. Then projects. Then tasks. Then PDFs. Then health records. Then account lists. Then receipts. Then manuals. Then some weird idea that the whole mess can be redeemed if the folder structure is finally correct this time.

That last part is the tell.

The more I have used Obsidian, the less interesting the question "what can it do?" has become. The more useful question has been: what should it not be?

Because it can do a lot. That does not mean it should.

## The question that matters more

The cleanest answer I have found is that Obsidian should be a wiki for notes.

That sounds almost too small, but I think that is why it works.

It should be where I think, write, connect ideas, keep reference material, and build project context. It should be where raw information turns into something shaped by me. My words, my summaries, my questions, my conclusions, my weird half-finished thinking that later becomes a better note or a blog post.

It should not be my records system.

Not my account inventory.
Not my health admin dashboard.
Not the place where every important PDF goes to die in an aesthetically pleasing folder.

That distinction cleared up more for me than any vault reorg ever has.

## The organizing trap

One of the best lines I have saved on this topic is that organizing is a black hole.

That is exactly how it feels when the system starts winning.

You tell yourself you are cleaning things up so future-you can work faster. Then an hour disappears renaming folders. Then another hour disappears trying to decide whether a note belongs in `finance`, `personal`, `config`, or some cleaner new abstraction that feels more correct. Then you start adjusting the structure around edge cases instead of around how you actually work.

The work itself never quite starts.

What makes this trap nasty is that it does not feel like procrastination. It feels responsible. It feels thoughtful. It feels like maintenance. Sometimes it even is maintenance. But past a certain point it becomes a very respectable way to avoid ambiguity.

If you (like me) already have an itch to organize the world, a tool like Obsidian can turn that itch into a hobby and call it a productivity system.

## The graph view problem

I have also fallen into what I think of as the graph view trap.

Not because the graph is evil or because linking is bad. Linking is great. I still do it constantly.

The trap is subtler than that.

You start taking notes constantly because the act of capturing and linking gives you the feeling that you are building understanding. Sometimes you are. Sometimes you are just making more surfaces to maintain.

There is a line I saved that I keep coming back to: paper does not think. It may help you think, but it does not think.

That gets at the whole problem.

Notes are useful when they help thought happen. They are much less useful when they become a substitute for contact with the thing itself. Work. Reading. Writing. Building. Making the decision. Having the uncomfortable conversation. Actually learning the topic instead of building a gorgeous metadata wrapper around it.

You can end up with the cleanest notes in the world and still be weirdly underprepared for the thing you were allegedly preparing for.

## Notes vs records

This is why I no longer like the old rule of "if you would be upset if it disappeared, put it in Obsidian."

It sounds sensible. It is also too broad.

Lots of important things do not belong in a wiki.

A blood test result is important.
A passport number is important.
A service receipt is important.
A list of email accounts is important.

None of those become better just because they have backlinks.

Importance is not the test. The test is what kind of information it is.

If it helps me think, it is a note.

If it helps me retrieve, it is a record.

That is the split.

Once I started using that rule, the app split got much simpler too:

- Reminders for actionable tasks
- Obsidian for notes
- Apple Notes for personal records and admin material

Apple Notes is not the point, really. You could swap it for something else. The important part is refusing to make the wiki carry jobs that belong to a different kind of system.

## What to do with PDFs

This also changed how I think about PDFs.

A PDF in Obsidian is not automatically a note. Most of the time it is just a file sitting near notes, pretending proximity is meaning.

Some PDFs absolutely belong there:

- papers I am actively studying
- manuals I genuinely revisit
- reference docs tied to a live project
- cheat sheets that support real work

But a huge amount of PDF clutter is just paperwork with better typography.

Tax slips.
Warranty documents.
Receipts.
Applications.
Coverage details.
Scanned records.

Those are not wiki material just because they came through my life.

User manuals sit in the middle. Some belong. A lot do not.

If a manual is a working reference, keep it.
If it is archival paperwork, move it out.

The useful pattern is usually not "save the full manual forever."

It is:

- keep the file if needed
- write the tiny note you actually need
- throw the rest of the mental burden away

That little distilled note is the wiki object. The manual is just source material.

## When the tool becomes the hobby

I think a lot of Obsidian discourse misses this because it is still stuck in capability mode.

Can you run your life from Obsidian? Sure.
Can you track everything there? Sure.
Can you clip, tag, embed, query, cross-link, publish, and graph your way into a small private civilization? Also yes.

But capability is a terrible stopping rule.

At some point you have to ask whether the tool is helping you achieve something or whether the tool itself has quietly become the hobby.

Both can be fine, to be clear. I am not morally opposed to nerding out over a note-taking app. I do it too. The problem starts when the hobby puts on a fake moustache and calls itself productivity.

That is when you get systems that expand faster than the life they are supposed to support.

## A Lot of Organization Is Anxiety in Better Typography

The part that interests me most now is that over-organization is not always about neatness.

Sometimes it is about control.

Not in some melodramatic way. Just in the ordinary way. Ambiguity is uncomfortable. Open loops are uncomfortable. Life admin is annoying. Unclear priorities are annoying. A vault reorg offers a temporary feeling that all of this can be made governable if you just classify it hard enough.

That feeling is real. It is also cheap.

It gives you motion without demanding contact with the harder thing.

So I think the real test for a system is pretty brutal:

- does it make retrieval easier?
- does it make thinking easier?
- does it reduce future decisions?
- or does it mostly satisfy the urge to arrange things?

Once the last one becomes the dominant payoff, the system has gone off the rails no matter how elegant it looks.

## What Obsidian Should Be

Obsidian is still one of the most useful tools I use.

I write in it. I think in it. I publish from it. I keep project context there. I keep reference notes there. I keep the distilled parts of what I learn there.

That is already a big enough job.

It does not need to become my filing cabinet, admin console, wallet, document graveyard, or emotional support database.

It is better when it stays a place where thinking happens.

That is enough.

## Unless it actually is the hobby

Not everything that looks like procrastination is procrastination.

There is a whole other kind of Obsidian user who is not confused about what they are doing at all. They are not avoiding work while pretending to maintain a system. They are just... enjoying themselves. Tinkering with templates. Adjusting CSS. Trying a new plugin. Reorganizing in silence on a day off with no music on because that is, genuinely, a nice way to spend a day off.

One comment I saved puts it better than I could:

> *Obsidian cured my destructive tendency to spend far too much time modding Skyrim by giving me the opportunity to spend far too much time modding my text editor.*

The problem is not the tinkering. The problem is when the tinkering puts on a fake moustache and calls itself a productivity system. If you know it is a hobby and you are fine with that, the whole warning dissolves. Tinker on.
